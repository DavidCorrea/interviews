# Interview: Priya Sharma — Non-Native English Speaker

**Persona:** Priya Sharma, 29, Software Engineer  
**Condition:** Non-native English speaker (Hindi is first language; English is second language)  
**Task:** Find what LaunchPad Lab does and how to contact them, to evaluate as a development partner for a side project.  
**Device/Setup:** Standard laptop browser, no assistive technology. Occasionally uses browser translation tools but prefers reading in English. May increase font size for readability.

---

## The Experience

I am looking for a company that can help me build a side project — a web application. A colleague recommended I look at LaunchPad Lab. I want to understand what this company does and whether they can build what I need, and then find how to contact them. I open the website in my browser.

### Landing on the Homepage

The page loads and I see a large headline at the top: **"AI Innovation. Digital Solutions. Results that Matter."**

I read this a few times. "AI Innovation" — okay, they work with AI. "Digital Solutions" — they make software of some kind. "Results that Matter" — this is a marketing phrase. It does not tell me anything specific. It is catchy but vague. I still do not know exactly what they do. Do they build websites? Mobile apps? Do they do consulting? This headline gives me a feeling but not information.

Below the headline is a longer sentence: **"LaunchPad Lab helps organizations harness the power of AI through tailored consulting and product development services. From strategy to execution, we build high-impact digital products that modernize operations, reduce costs, and unlock growth."**

This is better but I have to read it slowly. "Harness the power of AI" — "harness" is a word I know from Hindi where we say something like "use effectively," but this feels like a metaphor. "Tailored consulting" — "tailored" means customized, like a tailor makes clothes to fit, I think. "High-impact digital products" — I understand "digital products" but "high-impact" is vague marketing language. "Modernize operations" — okay, they help companies update their systems. "Reduce costs, and unlock growth" — "unlock growth" is another metaphor. You do not literally unlock growth with a key. It means they help businesses grow. I can figure this out, but it takes me extra effort. Every sentence has one or two phrases where I stop and think about the meaning.

I understand after reading this that they are a software company that builds digital products and does AI work. But the language is not simple. A clearer version would be: "We are a software company that builds web and mobile applications. We also help businesses use AI."

### The Navigation Menu

I look at the top navigation to orient myself. The menu items are: **Services, Technologies, Industries, Our Work, About Us, Insights, Contact.**

Most of these are clear to me. "Services" — good, this will tell me what they offer. "About Us" — standard, I know this. "Contact" — perfect, this is exactly the word I am looking for. These are common, literal labels that I can understand immediately.

But **"Insights"** — what does this mean? In my head, "insights" means "understanding" or "knowledge." Is this their blog? A research section? Reports? I am not sure. In India, company websites usually say "Blog" or "News" or "Resources." "Insights" is a softer, more creative English word that does not immediately tell me what I will find there. I would prefer "Blog" or "Articles."

**"Our Work"** is also slightly ambiguous to me. Does it mean their portfolio? Their projects? Their case studies? In context I can guess it means examples of their past projects, but "Portfolio" or "Case Studies" would be more explicit.

**"Technologies"** — this is clear. I expect to see a list of programming languages and tools they use.

When I hover over **"Services,"** a large dropdown menu opens. It shows six categories:

1. AI Automation
2. Web App Development
3. Mobile App Development
4. Salesforce Development
5. Product Strategy & Design
6. Managed Services & Support

This is very helpful. These labels are mostly clear and literal. "Web App Development" — yes, this is what I need. "Mobile App Development" — clear. "Salesforce Development" — I know what Salesforce is. "AI Automation" — clear enough.

However, there is also a paragraph of text inside the dropdown: **"Our cross-functional teams have the skills and experience to support the full digital product lifecycle, using proven methods to deliver more better and faster with AI at the core."**

I read this and I struggle. "Cross-functional teams" — I have heard this term at work but it is jargon. It means teams with people who have different skills, like designers and developers together. But the phrase itself is not self-explanatory. "Full digital product lifecycle" — this is technical jargon meaning they help from start to finish. "Deliver more better and faster" — this phrase feels grammatically awkward to me. "More better" is not standard English. I am confused whether this is intentional marketing language or an error. "AI at the core" — metaphorical. AI is at the center of what they do.

The **Industries** dropdown shows nine categories in three columns: Financial Services, Healthcare, Professional Services, Insurance, Technology, Legal, Private Equity, Education, Social Impact. These are clear English words and I can understand them all. Good.

### Trying to Understand What They Do

I scroll down the homepage. The next section is headed **"Your Strategic AI Partner"** with a subheading: **"AI-Powered Digital Solutions for the Modern Enterprise."**

"Strategic AI Partner" — I understand each word individually but together it feels like corporate speak. A simpler phrase would be "We help companies use AI." The word "enterprise" means large business, but in everyday English it is somewhat formal. In India we would more commonly say "company" or "organization."

Below this is a paragraph: **"At LaunchPad Lab, we combine deep expertise in product design and development with cutting-edge AI consulting to solve critical business challenges. We've helped hundreds of businesses across all industries solve complex problems that go beyond just ROI."**

Many phrases here require me to pause:
- **"Deep expertise"** — how deep? This is a vague intensifier.
- **"Cutting-edge AI consulting"** — "cutting-edge" is an idiom meaning very modern or advanced. I know this idiom but many non-native speakers might not.
- **"Critical business challenges"** — "critical" could mean important or dangerous. In this context it means important, but there is ambiguity.
- **"Go beyond just ROI"** — "ROI" is an acronym for "Return on Investment." It is never spelled out or explained. I know this term because I work in software, but a non-native speaker from a different industry might not.

The next major section is **"Driving ROI with AI"** with a heading: **"Make AI Your Competitive Advantage."**

"Competitive Advantage" — I know this phrase from business English classes, but it is not plain language. It means "something that helps you be better than your competitors." A simpler heading would be "How AI Helps Your Business Win."

Below this are the six service cards again, but this time with short taglines:
- AI Automation & Agentic AI: **"Enabling an autonomous digital workforce"**
- Web Application Development: **"Bespoke, redefining experiences"**
- Mobile Application Development: **"Meeting users where they are"**
- Salesforce Development: **"Solving business problems"**
- Product Strategy & Design: **"Agile, cross-functional expertise"**
- Managed Services & Support: **"Operating as an extension of your team"**

Several of these taglines are difficult for me:
- **"Enabling an autonomous digital workforce"** — "autonomous digital workforce" is heavy jargon. It means AI tools that work on their own like employees. But this phrase is not something I would understand immediately.
- **"Bespoke, redefining experiences"** — "Bespoke" is a British English word meaning custom-made. I had to learn this word specifically — it is not commonly used in Indian English or in everyday American English. It feels like an unusual choice.
- **"Meeting users where they are"** — this is an English idiom. It means making apps available on devices people already use (phones). But literally, "meeting users where they are" sounds like going to a physical location.
- **"Agile, cross-functional expertise"** — two jargon terms together. "Agile" in software means a specific methodology, but the word itself means quick and nimble. "Cross-functional" I explained earlier.
- **"Operating as an extension of your team"** — "extension of your team" is a metaphor meaning they work as if they are part of your company. Clear enough once I think about it, but not immediately literal.

Each "Learn More" link below the cards is identical. All six say "Learn More" with no differentiation. If I want to learn more about Web App Development specifically, I have to carefully match the "Learn More" link to its card. This is fine visually, but if I were scanning quickly, I might click the wrong one.

### The Problems We Solve Section

Further down I see **"Ship Game-Changing Digital Products to Market Fast."**

This sentence has two problems for me:
1. **"Ship"** — in software, "ship" means to release or launch a product. But the common English meaning of "ship" is a boat, or to send a package. I know the software meaning, but many non-native speakers from non-tech backgrounds would not understand "ship a product."
2. **"Game-Changing"** — this is an idiom meaning revolutionary or transformative. It comes from sports. Literally, it makes no sense in this context — there is no game being changed.

Below this are six benefit cards: Operational Efficiency, Modern Technology, Customer Engagement, Scalable Solutions, Expansive Resources, Speed to Market. The descriptions under each are short and mostly clear, though "CSAT" under Customer Engagement is another unexplained acronym (Customer Satisfaction). I have to guess its meaning from context.

### Statistics Section

The statistics section shows: **"12+ Years in Business," "730+ Projects," "240+ Clients."**

This is excellent. Numbers are universal. I do not need to translate or interpret them. This section communicates credibility instantly. The descriptions underneath are brief and clear. This is the kind of content that works very well for me.

### The Process Section

**"Accelerate Digital Product Speed to Market"** — "accelerate" is a formal word for "make faster." The heading is understandable but wordy. "Our Signature Approach" above it is a creative label — "signature" here means unique or characteristic, not a handwritten name. I can parse this but it requires a moment.

The process steps are: **Discover** (Research & Define), **Deliver** (Development, QA Testing), **Release** (Deploy, Ongoing Support).

These are clear and well-structured. Short labels with brief explanations. The use of familiar software development terminology (QA, deploy, sprint) is fine for me as a software engineer, though a non-technical non-native speaker would struggle with terms like "bi-weekly sprint releases" and "UAT validation."

### Finding Contact Information

I click **"Contact"** in the navigation. This is a clear, explicit label and I find it immediately. Good.

The contact page headline says: **"Ready to Build Something Great?"**

This is a promotional question, not an informational heading. It does not say "Contact Us" or "Get in Touch." As a non-native speaker, I can infer that this is the contact page because I clicked the "Contact" link, but if I had landed here from a search engine, the heading alone would not confirm that this is a contact page. A heading like "Contact Us" or "Send Us a Message" would be clearer.

Below the heading: **"Complete the form below to tell us about your project. We will get in touch with you as soon as possible."** This is clear and direct. I appreciate the simple instruction.

However, where the form should be, I see: **"Notice: JavaScript is required for this content."** The form is not loading. I think my browser may be blocking something. This is frustrating — the primary contact method does not work without JavaScript. There is no alternative immediately visible, like an email address next to the broken form.

I scroll down further. There is a testimonials section with client quotes. The testimonials use very idiomatic American English:
- **"any crazy idea we cook up, you guys can really digest"** — "cook up" means to create or think of (idiom from cooking), "digest" means to process or understand (metaphor from eating). This is highly figurative.
- **"a mainstay in our company's growth"** — "mainstay" means a main support. Not a common word in everyday English.
- **"what could otherwise become a tumultuous journey of entrepreneurship"** — "tumultuous" is a difficult vocabulary word meaning chaotic or stormy. "Journey of entrepreneurship" is a metaphor.

I understand the general meaning — clients are happy — but parsing each quote takes effort because of the idioms and figurative language.

Below the testimonials, I finally find the **"Let's Connect"** section with:
- **Email:** contact@launchpadlab.com
- **Call:** (312) 888-9651
- **Connect with Us on LinkedIn:** Connect

This is exactly what I needed. Clear, literal labels. Email, phone number, LinkedIn. The email address and phone number are factual information that requires no interpretation. The word "Connect" as the LinkedIn link label is slightly vague (connect to what?) but in context it is clear.

However, I had to scroll past a broken form and a long testimonials section to find this. If I had given up earlier, I would have missed it.

### The Footer

The footer also contains useful information:
- **"LaunchPad Lab is a Chicago-based digital product firm that builds high-impact digital products that drive business results."** — This is a one-sentence summary. "Chicago-based" — clear. "Digital product firm" — clear. "High-impact" — marketing language but acceptable. "Drive business results" — another metaphor (driving like a car), but I understand it.
- Address: **2045 W Grand Ave Ste BPMB 37406, Chicago, Illinois 60612**
- Phone: **(312) 888-9651**
- Social links: **Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn**
- Footer navigation: Services, Technologies, Industries, Our Work, About Us, Insights, Careers, Client Testimonials

The footer is well-organized and clear. The address and phone number are presented without any metaphorical language. This is helpful.

### The Services Page

I also visit the Services page to understand their offerings better. The headline is **"AI-Centered Digital Product Development"** and the subheading is **"Our cross-functional teams have the skills and experience to support the full digital product lifecycle, using proven methods to deliver more better and faster with AI at the core."**

Again, "deliver more better and faster" is confusing grammar for me. "More better" is not standard English — it should be "better" or "more effectively." This makes me wonder if there is a typo, which reduces my confidence in the professionalism of the content.

Further down, I find **"Your 3-Step Blueprint for What to Build"** which introduces something called **"Discovery Space Navigator."** This is a proprietary name that means nothing to me. It continues: "Just fill out a short form, and we'll run your inputs through Mission Control—our proprietary AI tool built to pinpoint the highest-impact opportunities."

"Mission Control" — another proprietary name, and also a cultural reference to NASA space missions. I recognize the reference but it adds no clarity. "Pinpoint the highest-impact opportunities" — "pinpoint" means to identify precisely (metaphor from a pin's point), "highest-impact" is corporate jargon.

There is also a FAQ section on the services page that is actually very helpful. The questions and answers use relatively clear language and address practical concerns: how much things cost, how long projects take, whether the team is US-based. This is the most useful and clearly written content I have found on the site.

### What Worked Well

- **"Contact" in the navigation** is clearly labeled and I found it immediately. This is the single most important thing for me.
- **Service category labels** (Web App Development, Mobile App Development, etc.) are clear and descriptive.
- **The statistics section** (12+ years, 730+ projects, 240+ clients) communicates credibility without any language barriers.
- **Direct contact information** (email, phone) on the contact page is factual and requires no interpretation.
- **The footer** is well-organized with clear navigation links and company information.
- **The FAQ section** on the services page uses relatively plain language.
- **Navigation labels** like "Services," "About Us," and "Contact" are explicit and standard.
- **Industry names** in the Industries dropdown are clear and recognizable.

### What Did Not Work

- **Heavy use of jargon:** "cross-functional teams," "full digital product lifecycle," "bespoke," "agentic AI," "autonomous digital workforce," "ROI," "CSAT," "UAT," "sprint releases" — none of these are explained.
- **Frequent idioms and metaphors:** "harness the power," "cutting-edge," "ship game-changing products," "unlock growth," "meeting users where they are," "cook up," "digest," "mainstay," "tumultuous journey," "drive results," "competitive advantage."
- **Creative/promotional headings instead of informational ones:** "Ready to Build Something Great?" instead of "Contact Us." "Ship Game-Changing Digital Products to Market Fast" instead of "Problems We Solve."
- **Inconsistent contact terminology:** The site uses five different phrases to mean "contact us": "Contact" (nav), "Connect with an Expert" (homepage CTA), "Reach Out" (contact page label), "Let's Connect" (contact page section), and "Let's Build Something Great" (services page CTA). This is confusing. Which one is the contact page? Which one is a sales pitch? I cannot be sure they all lead to the same place.
- **"Insights" instead of "Blog"** — ambiguous for a non-native speaker.
- **"More better and faster"** — awkward grammar that could be a typo or intentional but is confusing either way.
- **"Bespoke"** — an uncommon British English word used without explanation.
- **Contact form requires JavaScript** with no fallback contact method near the form.
- **Testimonials use highly idiomatic American English** that is difficult to parse.
- **Proprietary names** ("Discovery Space Navigator," "Mission Control") are meaningless without context and add cognitive load.
- **Dense paragraphs** throughout the site use compound sentences with multiple metaphors per sentence.

### Did I Achieve My Goals?

**Goal 1: Find what LaunchPad Lab does.** Yes — but it took more effort than it should have. The service category labels (Web App Development, Mobile App Development, Salesforce Development, etc.) were clear and gave me what I needed. But the surrounding text was filled with jargon, idioms, and marketing metaphors that slowed me down. If the entire site were written at the clarity level of the service labels and the FAQ section, it would have been much easier. I understand they are a Chicago-based company that builds web applications, mobile applications, and works with Salesforce and AI. They have been in business since 2012 and have worked with large companies like Harvard and Whirlpool.

**Goal 2: Find how to contact them.** Yes — but with unnecessary friction. The "Contact" link in the navigation was clear and easy to find. But the contact page itself had a broken form (JavaScript required), a promotional heading instead of a clear "Contact Us," and the actual email and phone number were hidden below testimonials. The direct contact information — contact@launchpadlab.com and (312) 888-9651 — was exactly what I needed, but I had to scroll past barriers to find it.

### Would I Leave the Site?

I would not leave the site, because ultimately I was able to find the information I needed. But I would hesitate to recommend this site to a friend who speaks less English than I do. The language difficulty is not at the level where I cannot use the site — it is at the level where everything takes 30-50% longer to understand than it should. Every paragraph has one or two phrases where I stop, re-read, and mentally translate. Over the course of a full site visit, this adds up to significant extra cognitive effort.

If I were comparing this company to a competitor whose website used simpler, more literal language, the competitor would immediately feel more approachable and trustworthy to me — not because their work is better, but because I could understand what they do without having to decode marketing metaphors. Clear language is not just an accessibility feature. For someone like me, it is a trust signal.
