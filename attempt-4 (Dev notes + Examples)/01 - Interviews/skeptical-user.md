# Skeptical User Interview: Derek Walsh

**Persona**: Derek Walsh, 45, Procurement Manager  
**Condition**: Skepticism — actively evaluates website trustworthiness before engaging  
**Date**: February 16, 2026  
**Website Tested**: https://launchpadlab.com/  
**Goals**: (1) Find what LaunchPad Lab does, (2) Find how to contact them, (3) Evaluate trustworthiness  

---

## First Impressions

I landed on the homepage and my first thought was: this looks polished. The design is clean, the layout is professional, and the blue color scheme gives it a corporate, established feel. The hero section loads with a background image and the headline "AI Innovation. Digital Solutions. Results that Matter." Immediately, though, my antennae go up. "Results that Matter" is the kind of vague tagline every agency uses. It tells me nothing specific. But I keep reading.

The subtext below the headline is better: "LaunchPad Lab helps organizations harness the power of AI through tailored consulting and product development services. From strategy to execution, we build high-impact digital products that modernize operations, reduce costs, and unlock growth." Okay — that's more concrete. They're an AI consulting and product development firm. I can work with that.

There's a prominent blue button: "Connect with an Expert." It's clearly visible and links to the contact page. No issue finding where to go if I wanted to reach out. That's a point in their favor — they're not hiding the contact option behind three layers of navigation.

## Evaluating What They Do

Scrolling down the homepage, I find a section labeled "Your Strategic AI Partner" with the heading "AI-Powered Digital Solutions for the Modern Enterprise." There's an embedded Wistia video about their approach. I appreciate that they offer a video — it shows they're willing to show their process, not just write marketing copy. The text says they've "helped hundreds of businesses across all industries solve complex problems that go beyond just ROI." That's a strong claim. I want to see proof.

Below this is a cards section titled "Make AI Your Competitive Advantage" under a "Driving ROI with AI" label. The six service cards are: AI Automation & Agentic AI, Web Application Development, Mobile Application Development, Salesforce Development, Product Strategy & Design, and Managed Services & Support. Each has a one-liner description. The descriptions are brief — "Enabling an autonomous digital workforce," "Bespoke, redefining experiences," "Meeting users where they are," "Solving business problems," "Agile, cross-functional expertise," "Operating as an extension of your team." These are vague. "Solving business problems" under Salesforce Development tells me absolutely nothing. "Bespoke, redefining experiences" is marketing fluff. I would have preferred concrete descriptions like "We build custom React and Ruby on Rails web applications for mid-market enterprises" — something that tells me what they actually deliver.

The navigation mega menu is more helpful. Under "Services," I can see the full list of offerings with icons. Under "Industries," they list Financial Services, Healthcare, Professional Services, Insurance, Technology, Legal, Private Equity, Education, and Social Impact. That's a broad range but at least they're specific about who they serve.

The "Problems We Solve" tab section includes items like Operational Efficiency, Modern Technology, Customer Engagement, Scalable Solutions, Expansive Resources, and Speed to Market. Each tab has a short blurb. These are reasonable categories but they read like a generic consulting pitch. Every agency claims to deliver operational efficiency and speed to market. The tab images appear to be stock-style illustrations, not screenshots of actual client work.

## Trust Signals: What I Found

This is where things get more interesting, and where LaunchPad Lab starts earning some credibility.

**Client Logos**: The homepage shows a scrolling banner of client logos: Kawasaki Engines, 1-800-GOT-JUNK?, Harvard University, Prosci, Whirlpool, Northwestern University, CDK Global, Lurie Children's Hospital of Chicago, Indiana University, Select Home Warranty, MD Anderson Cancer Center, and Bullhorn. These are real, recognizable companies and institutions. Harvard, Northwestern, MD Anderson Cancer Center — if those are legitimate clients, that's a significant trust signal. The logos aren't just generic tech startups; they include universities, hospitals, and Fortune 500 companies.

**Stats**: The homepage displays "12+ Years in Business," "730+ Projects," and "240+ Clients" with supporting text that says 90% of clients work with them for longer than one year. Those are solid numbers. The repeat client rate is particularly important — vendors who retain 90% of clients are either doing good work or have very sticky contracts, and both suggest stability.

**Case Studies with Metrics**: There's a case study carousel with real data:
- **Prosci**: "360-Customer View in Salesforce" — 5,400+ current users, 3,200+ unique projects created, 30+ countries using the tools.
- **CDK Global**: "Sales Discovery Portal for Automotive Companies" — 90% reduction in training time, improved sales team alignment, streamlined discovery and quoting process.
- **MTC**: "Spurring Enterprise Innovation" — 3 new innovative digital products.

These include company names, specific project descriptions, and measurable outcomes. That's exactly what I look for. The Prosci case study with 5,400+ users and 30+ countries is the kind of concrete evidence that marketing copy alone can't fabricate.

**Testimonials**: On the contact page, there's a testimonial carousel with real quotes and real attributions:
- Patrick West, Vice President of Apex Leaders
- Amplify Credit Union (company name, no individual)
- Kalyan Ray-Mazumder, Founder & CEO of Prepmedians
- Cassi Tucker, Manager of Museum Technology, Eskenazi Museum of Art
- Jeremy Eddie, Senior VP of Finance & Administration and CFO of Omaha Zoo

These include real names, real titles, and real companies. The Jeremy Eddie quote mentions a measurable result: "a 7% increase in both online membership sales and general admission tickets." That's verifiable. One testimonial (Amplify Credit Union) lacks an individual name, which is less convincing, but overall the attribution quality is above average.

**Awards and Certifications**: On the About page, there's a scrolling banner of award badges: Top Salesforce Company 2023 (Clutch), Top Software Developers United States 2023 (Clutch), Clutch 1000 (multiple years), Best Places to Work Chicago, and a Salesforce Partner badge in the footer. The Clutch badges are third-party validated. In the footer, there's also an AgencyReview.dev widget showing a 10.0 rating and a Clutch widget. These are independently verifiable.

**Leadership Team**: The About page has a "Meet Our Leadership Team" section showing 15 team members with photos, names, titles, and links to their LinkedIn profiles. I can see Scott Weisman (Co-Founder & CEO), Ryan Francis (Partner, President, & Head of Sales), Brendan Hennessy (Co-Founder & CTO), Monique Silva (VP of Design), and others. Every person links to their LinkedIn. That's a strong transparency signal — I can verify these people exist, check their experience, and see if their backgrounds match the company's claims.

**Physical Address and Phone**: The footer on the contact page lists "2045 W Grand Ave Ste B, PMB 37406, Chicago, Illinois 60612" and phone number (312) 888-9651. There's also an email: contact@launchpadlab.com. A PMB (Private Mailbox) address is slightly less impressive than a proper office lease — it suggests either a WeWork-style arrangement or a fully remote team using a mail forwarding service — but the phone number and email are present and not hidden.

## Red Flags and Concerns

**Vague Service Descriptions**: The service card descriptions on the homepage are too fluffy. "Enabling an autonomous digital workforce" and "Bespoke, redefining experiences" sound like they were written by a marketing team, not a technical team. A procurement manager like me wants specifics: what technologies, what timelines, what engagement models.

**No Pricing Transparency**: There's zero information about pricing, engagement models, or even ballpark ranges. I understand consulting firms rarely publish rates, but even a "projects typically start at $X" or "we work on time-and-materials or fixed-bid" would help me gauge whether they're in my budget before I fill out a form.

**Contact Form is JavaScript-Dependent**: The contact form on the /contact page requires JavaScript to render (Ninja Forms). If JavaScript fails to load, you see "Notice: JavaScript is required for this content." The form asks for First Name, Last Name, Company Email, Company, and "How Can We Help You?" — which is reasonable. But there's a reCAPTCHA, and the form has multiple hidden UTM tracking fields. The tracking isn't a deal-breaker but it does mean they're heavily invested in marketing attribution.

**"AI" Saturation**: The site leans heavily into AI messaging. Nearly every section mentions AI. The title is "AI-Centric Digital Product Design & Development." The meta description says "AI at the center of all we do." For someone who has been burned by vendors chasing trends, this reads like they've recently pivoted hard to AI positioning. Their founding year is 2012, long before the current AI wave. I'd want to know: is this a genuine competency or a marketing rebrand?

**Some Images Missing Alt Text**: Several images across the site have empty alt attributes (alt=""). While the decorative icons appropriately use empty alt text, some substantive images — like the about page team photos ("alt=''") and certain award badges — are missing descriptive alt text. This isn't a trust concern for me personally, but it signals less attention to detail than I'd expect from a premium digital product firm.

**Inconsistent Stats**: The homepage says "12+ Years in Business" while the About page says "13+ Years in Business." A small discrepancy, but it suggests the content isn't maintained consistently across pages. If they can't keep their own stats aligned, what does that say about attention to detail on client projects?

**Footer Typo**: The footer phone number link has an extra quotation mark in the HTML: `href="tel:312-888-9651""`. This is a minor HTML error but it's the kind of thing a quality-focused development firm should catch on their own site.

## Attempting to Contact

I navigated to the contact page. The page headline says "Ready to Build Something Great?" with "Complete the form below to tell us about your project. We will get in touch with you as soon as possible." Below the form, there's a "Let's Connect" section with three cards: Email (contact@launchpadlab.com), Call ((312) 888-9651), and Connect with Us on LinkedIn. 

The contact information is clear and accessible. I have a phone number, an email, and a LinkedIn page. The form requires JavaScript, which is a minor concern, but the alternative contact methods (email and phone) are visible without needing the form to load.

## Final Trust Decision

After a thorough evaluation, **I would proceed cautiously**. LaunchPad Lab passes my trust bar — but just barely, and with some reservations.

**What convinced me:**
- Real client logos from recognizable institutions (Harvard, Northwestern, MD Anderson, Whirlpool)
- Case studies with measurable, specific outcomes (5,400+ users, 90% reduction in training time, 7% revenue increase)
- Testimonials with real names, titles, and companies
- Visible leadership team with LinkedIn links I can verify
- Third-party awards from Clutch and AgencyReview.dev
- Clear contact information including phone, email, and physical address
- 12+ years in business with 240+ clients and 90% retention rate

**What gives me pause:**
- Heavy AI marketing that feels like a recent positioning pivot
- Vague service descriptions that don't tell me what I'll actually get
- No pricing transparency
- Minor quality issues on their own site (inconsistent stats, HTML errors, missing alt text)
- PMB mailing address rather than a proper office
- Contact form requires JavaScript with no fallback

If this were a high-stakes procurement decision, I would reach out via email rather than the form, ask pointed questions about their AI capabilities versus their pre-AI track record, and request references from clients in my industry. The site gives me enough to start a conversation, but not enough to skip due diligence.
