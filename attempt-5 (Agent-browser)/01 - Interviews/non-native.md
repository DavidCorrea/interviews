# Interview: Yuki Tanaka — Non-Native English Speaker

**Persona:** Yuki Tanaka, 41, Operations Director, Osaka, Japan  
**English Level:** Intermediate (B1-B2 CEFR)  
**Context:** Looking for a US-based technology partner to build an inventory management application for a Japanese manufacturing company expanding into the US market.  
**Date:** February 18, 2026  
**Website:** https://launchpadlab.com/

---

## Landing on the Homepage

I open the LaunchPad Lab website. The first thing I see is a large photo of people sitting around a table with laptops. They look like they are having a meeting. The image is nice — it looks professional and like a real team, not stock photography. This gives me a good first impression.

The main heading says: **"AI Innovation. Digital Solutions. Results that Matter."** I read this carefully. "AI Innovation" — I know what AI is, artificial intelligence. "Digital Solutions" — okay, they make computer solutions. "Results that Matter" — this is a common English phrase, I understand it but it feels like a slogan, not real information. After reading this heading, I still do not know exactly what this company does. Do they make software? Do they sell AI products? Are they a consulting company? The heading is too vague.

Below the heading there is a paragraph: *"LaunchPad Lab helps organizations harness the power of AI through tailored consulting and product development services. From strategy to execution, we build high-impact digital products that modernize operations, reduce costs, and unlock growth."*

I need to read this sentence twice. "Harness the power" — this is a metaphor, like controlling a horse. I understand it but it takes extra thinking. "Tailored consulting" — okay, custom consulting. "Product development services" — they build products. This is starting to make more sense. "High-impact digital products" — "high-impact" is a marketing word, it does not tell me anything specific. "Modernize operations, reduce costs, and unlock growth" — "unlock growth" is another metaphor. Growth is not a lock. I need to translate this mentally: they help companies update their systems, save money, and grow. Okay.

The button says **"Connect with an Expert"** — this is clear enough. It is a way to contact them.

### Client Logos

Below the hero section, I see **"Trusted by Hundreds of Innovative Businesses"** with logos of companies like Kawasaki, Whirlpool, Harvard University, and others. This is very helpful! I recognize Kawasaki immediately — this is a Japanese company. And Whirlpool is a manufacturing company. This tells me they work with companies similar to mine. The logos communicate more to me than the paragraphs of text above. I wish there were more visual elements like this on the site.

### "AI-Powered Digital Solutions for the Modern Enterprise"

I scroll down. Another heading: **"AI-Powered Digital Solutions for the Modern Enterprise."** I pause. "Modern Enterprise" — enterprise means big company, I think. This section's paragraph is very long and very dense:

*"At LaunchPad Lab, we combine deep expertise in product design and development with cutting-edge AI consulting to solve critical business challenges."*

"Cutting-edge" — this is an idiom. I have heard it before but I need a moment to remember it means "very advanced." "Cross-functional teams" — I do not know what this means. Cross... functional? Teams that work across different functions? This is industry jargon that I would need to look up. "ROI" appears without explanation — I know this means Return on Investment from my business experience, but it should not be assumed that every reader knows this abbreviation.

### Services Section: "Make AI Your Competitive Advantage"

The services section heading says **"Make AI Your Competitive Advantage."** "Competitive advantage" — I know this phrase from business school, but it is not simple English.

The service cards have short descriptions:

- **"AI Automation & Agentic AI"** — what is "Agentic AI"? I have never heard this term. "Enabling an autonomous digital workforce" — a digital workforce? Workers that are digital? This is very confusing metaphorical language.
- **"Web Application Development"** — this is clear. But the tagline says "Bespoke, redefining experiences." "Bespoke" is a very difficult English word. I think it means custom-made? And "redefining experiences" is marketing language that tells me nothing specific.
- **"Mobile Application Development"** — clear title. "Meeting users where they are" — this is an English idiom that means being available on the device users prefer. But for me, it literally sounds like meeting people at a location. Confusing.
- **"Product Strategy & Design"** — relatively clear. "Agile, cross-functional expertise" — "Agile" is a technical methodology term. Without context, it just sounds like the English word meaning "quick." "Cross-functional" appears again without explanation.
- **"Managed Services & Support"** — "Operating as an extension of your team" — another metaphor. An extension, like extending a table? They mean they work like part of your company.

### "Ship Game-Changing Digital Products to Market Fast"

This heading is very difficult for me. "Ship" as a verb meaning "to send/deliver" — I know "ship" as a boat. I need a moment to understand they mean "deliver." "Game-changing" is an idiom from sports. So they are saying "deliver very important digital products quickly." Why not just say that?

The paragraph below uses the word **"bespoke"** again and **"forging a partnership"** — "forging" is a metaphor from metalworking. This is the third metaphor in this section alone.

There are tabs below: "Operational Efficiency," "Modern Technology," "Customer Engagement," "Scalable Solutions," "Expansive Resources," "Speed to Market." Some of these are clear, but "Expansive Resources" is confusing — expansive means large? What resources? And one tab mentions **"UI/UX"** and **"CSAT"** — I do not know what CSAT means. These abbreviations are not explained anywhere.

### Statistics Section

The numbers section is very helpful: **12+ Years in Business, 730+ Projects, 240+ Clients.** Numbers are universal. I do not need to translate them. This is the most effective communication on the entire page for me.

### "Our Signature Approach"

**"Our Signature Approach"** — "signature" in this context means "special" or "unique." The process steps — Discover, Deliver, Release — are somewhat clear, but the descriptions use jargon: "bi-weekly sprint releases," "UAT validation," "seamless handoff." I work in operations so I know some of these terms, but "UAT" is never explained (User Acceptance Testing, I think?), and "seamless handoff" uses two words that are both somewhat idiomatic.

### Contact Form on Homepage

At the bottom of the homepage, there is a form with the heading **"Let's Build Something Great."** This is informal — "Let's" is a contraction, "Build Something Great" is vague. But the form itself has clear labels: First Name, Last Name, Company Email, Company, How Can We Help You? These are all standard and understandable. The form is easy to use.

The footer has a phone number **(312) 888-9651** and an address in Chicago. This is clear. There is also a heading **"Let's Get Social"** with links to Facebook, Instagram, and LinkedIn. "Let's Get Social" is a casual English phrase — I understand it but it feels informal for a business website.

## Summary: Did I Achieve My Goals?

### Goal 1: Figure out what LaunchPad Lab does
**Partially achieved.** After reading multiple pages, I understand that LaunchPad Lab is a software development company in Chicago that builds web applications, mobile applications, and works with Salesforce. They also do AI consulting. However, it took me visiting four different pages and spending significant effort re-reading marketing language to piece this together. The homepage alone was insufficient — too much jargon and too many idioms obscured the core message.

### Goal 2: Find how to contact them
**Achieved.** The contact form appears on every page, the phone number and address are in the footer, and the "Contact" button is clearly visible in the navigation. This was the easiest part of my experience.

### What Worked
- **Client logos** were the most effective communication tool — I immediately recognized Kawasaki and Whirlpool
- **Numbers and statistics** (12+ years, 730+ projects, 240+ clients) are universally understandable
- **Case study titles** on the Our Work page were practical and descriptive
- **Contact form labels** were clear and standard
- **Leadership team** section built trust through concrete information
- **Navigation labels** were mostly clear (Services, Technologies, Our Work, About Us, Contact)

### What Did Not Work
- **Excessive idioms:** "cutting-edge," "game-changing," "unleash potential," "forging partnerships," "pulling ahead," "move the needle," "ship products"
- **Unexplained jargon:** ROI, CSAT, UAT, UI/UX, cross-functional, agile, bespoke, agentic AI
- **Marketing superlatives:** "world-class," "high-impact," "transformative," "mission-critical," "best-in-class"
- **Complex metaphors:** The entire space theme (LaunchPad, Mission Control, Navigator) adds cognitive load
- **Dense paragraphs** with nested clauses and passive voice
- **"Insights"** as a navigation label instead of "Blog" — unclear for non-native speakers
- **Casual tone** ("Let's Build Something Great," "We're obsessed with...") feels unprofessional to me culturally

### Overall Experience
As a non-native English speaker, I felt like this website was written for native English speakers who are already familiar with the US tech industry. The language barrier added significant friction to my experience. I could eventually understand what the company does, but only after visiting multiple pages and re-reading many paragraphs. If I were in a hurry, I might have left the site and looked for a company with simpler, more direct communication. The visual elements — logos, screenshots, numbers, team photos — were my best allies in understanding this company. More of those, and less marketing prose, would make this site much more accessible to people like me.
