### 5. What Would Increase Clarity

**Critical Improvements:**

1. **Darken All Body Text** (Most Important):
   - Change body text from light gray to black (#000000) or very dark gray (#333333 or #222222). This single change would make the biggest difference for users with contrast sensitivity issues. Text should not be #666666 or lighter - it's simply not dark enough.

2. **Increase Font Weight:**
   - Consider using a medium or semi-bold font weight for body text instead of regular/light. Thin fonts are harder to read, especially at smaller sizes and with low contrast. A slightly bolder font would help text stand out more clearly.

3. **Make Links Obviously Clickable:**
   - Add clear underlines to all text links, or use a significantly darker/brighter color that's unmistakable. When I hover over a link, make it bold or change the background color noticeably - not just a 10% color shift.

4. **Strengthen Service Box Descriptions:**
   - The subtitle text under each service ("Enabling an autonomous digital workforce," etc.) should be in darker text or a larger font size. These descriptions provide valuable context and shouldn't be so faint.

5. **Add Visible Borders to UI Elements:**
   - Service boxes, problem boxes, and other content containers need stronger borders. Use a medium gray (#999999 or darker) for borders instead of very light gray. This will help separate sections visually.

6. **Increase Contrast for Testimonials:**
   - Client testimonials should be in dark gray or black text, not light gray. These are important for building trust and should be easy to read.

7. **Make Form Fields Clearly Visible:**
   - If there are form fields on the contact page, they need dark borders (at least #999999 or darker) and clear labels in dark text. Placeholder text should also be readable, not super faint.

8. **Strengthen Hover and Focus States:**
   - When hovering over buttons or links, make the change dramatic: darken the background by at least 20%, add a clear border, or make the text bold. Subtle 5-10% color shifts are invisible to users with contrast sensitivity issues.

9. **Use Solid, Darker Icons:**
   - Replace very light, pastel-colored icons with darker, more saturated versions. Icons should have clear outlines and not rely on subtle color gradients.

10. **Add Visual Weight to Key Information:**
    - Important statistics, process steps, and feature descriptions should use bold text or larger font sizes in addition to darker colors. Don't rely solely on subtle color differences to establish hierarchy.

11. **Improve Logo Contrast:**
    - For client logos that are light-colored, consider adding a subtle dark border or placing them on a very light gray background (like #F8F8F8) instead of pure white to provide some definition.

12. **Test with Grayscale:**
    - A good test is to view the site in grayscale (black and white). If information disappears or becomes hard to distinguish without color, then the contrast is too low. Everything important should be visible in grayscale.

## Final Thoughts

I was able to complete my task - I figured out what LaunchPad Lab does and I found their contact information. However, the experience was visually exhausting in a way that it shouldn't be.

The fundamental problem is that this site uses a soft, minimalist design palette with lots of light gray text. This design aesthetic prioritizes visual elegance over readability. While it might look sophisticated on a high-quality monitor in perfect lighting conditions, it creates a significant barrier for users like me who have reduced contrast sensitivity.

Here's what happened in practice:
- I read all the headings easily (they're dark enough)
- I struggled through body text, often giving up halfway through paragraphs
- I missed subtle visual cues like link hover states entirely
- I felt uncertain about what was clickable and what wasn't
- My eyes felt fatigued after just a few minutes of browsing

The ironic thing is that the information is all there. The content seems good. The layout is logical. But the presentation - specifically the low contrast text - puts up an unnecessary wall between the content and the reader.

The fixes aren't complicated:
- Make text darker (use #333 or #000 instead of #666 or #777)
- Make interactive elements obviously interactive
- Use stronger borders and visual separators
- Ensure hover/focus states are dramatic, not subtle

**My rating: 4/10 for contrast sensitivity accessibility**

The structure is fine, but the visual execution makes reading feel like work. For a company that creates "engaging UI/UX" for their clients, the irony is that their own site's UI isn't very engaging for users with visual processing differences.

Make it darker. Make it bolder. Make it obvious. My eyes will thank you.
